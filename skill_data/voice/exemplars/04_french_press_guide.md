THE ULTIMATE GUIDE TO FRENCH PRESS COFFEE
Source: pullandpourcoffee.com | Author: Andrew Pautler | Date: April 30, 2021

BREW SPECS AT A GLANCE
- Grind: Coarse
- Brew Ratio: 1:12 (coffee to water)
- Water Temperature: 195–205°F (91–96°C)
- Total Brew Time: 4 minutes

---

WHY USE A FRENCH PRESS?
- Cost-effective: Less expensive than most other coffee brewers
- Flavor control: You set the brew time and water temp
- Deeper flavor: Metal mesh filter allows coffee oils through (paper filters trap oils, reducing richness)
- Portable: Light and easy to use anywhere

---

BREW INSTRUCTIONS (Step by Step)
1. Preheat the French Press and plunger with hot water for ~30 seconds. Discard water.
2. Grind coffee coarsely. Add to French Press, distributing evenly.
3. Pour half of the total water (200°F / 93.3°C) in a circular motion over the grounds, ensuring all grounds are submerged.
4. Let coffee bloom (rest) for ~30 seconds.
5. Slowly add remaining water. Put on lid; lower plunger until mesh just touches the water surface.
6. Set timer for 4 minutes.
7. Press the plunger down slowly over ~30 seconds.
8. Immediately pour into a mug or container to prevent over-extraction and bitterness.

---

RECIPE (Standard 2-Serving)
- Coffee: 32g, coarsely ground
- Water: 400g at 200°F (93.3°C)
- Ratio is scalable; maintain 1:12

---

TROUBLESHOOTING
- Tastes bad: Check water quality — poor water = poor coffee
- Weak or under-extracted: Water may be too cool; stay within 195–205°F range
- Too bitter: Grind may be too fine, or coffee brewed too long (decant immediately after pressing)
- Too much sediment: Press more slowly — take a full 30 seconds on the plunge
- Inconsistent results: Use a scale for accurate dosing; invest in a burr grinder for consistent grind size

---

SIZING GUIDE
- 3-cup French Press → ~12 oz (serves 1 mug)
- 8-cup French Press → ~34 oz (serves ~3 mugs)
- 12-cup French Press → ~51 oz (serves ~5 mugs)
Note: "Cup" sizes are based on 4 oz European cups, not 8–10 oz American mugs.

---

OTHER USES FOR A FRENCH PRESS
- Rehydrate dried foods
- Juice berries
- Mix drinks
- Rinse grains

---

BRIEF HISTORY
- Earliest legend: A Frenchman in the 1850s accidentally discovered the method.
- First official patent: Attilio Calimani and Giulio Moneta (Italian), 1929.
