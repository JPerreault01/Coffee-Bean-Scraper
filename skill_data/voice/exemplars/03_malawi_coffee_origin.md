EXPLORING MALAWI AS A COFFEE ORIGIN
Source: perfectdailygrind.com | Author: Peter Gakuo | Date: May 5, 2021

COUNTRY OVERVIEW
Malawi is a landlocked country in southeast Africa, bordered by Mozambique, Zambia, and Tanzania. Population: 19+ million. Economy is largely agricultural, supporting ~90% of the rural population. Main exports historically include tobacco and tea; coffee is a smaller but historically significant crop.

PRODUCTION HISTORY
- Coffee was introduced in the late 1800s when Dr. John Buchanan brought the Nyasaland variety (a Bourbon/Typica derivative) from the Royal Botanic Gardens in Edinburgh.
- Pre-independence (before 1964), farms were concentrated in the Thyolo and Mulanje regions, owned largely by British colonists.
- After independence (1971), the Smallholder Coffee Authority (SCA) was formed to organize the sector.
- Coffee wilt disease and organizational mismanagement in the 1980s–90s caused serious production declines.
- Peak export: ~109,000 60kg bags in 1993. Current output: ~9,000 bags/year.

CURRENT GROWING REGIONS
- Major estates remain in southern Malawi: Mangochi, Mulanje, Zomba, Thyolo.
- Only 5 active major coffee estates; coffee is not the main cash crop on most (tobacco, tea, and macadamia take priority).
- ~4,000 smallholder farms in northern and central Malawi; most have fewer than 200 plants each.
- Most estates use irrigation.

VARIETALS
- Arabica only.
- Catimor (replaced most Caturra between 1999–2007; rust-resistant)
- Caturra (formerly dominant)
- Catuai, K7, Ruiru 11, SL28, SL34
- Rarer: Geisha, Nyika 129, S. Agaro

FLAVOR PROFILE
Malawian coffee is generally described as sweet, delicate, and floral, with notes of liquorice and spice.

PROCESSING & HARVEST
- Harvest season: April to September
- Both washed and natural processing used
- Processing begins at local washing stations; secondary processing in Mzuzu (Northern Region capital)
- Total annual production: ~2,000 tonnes (smallholders + estates combined)

COOPERATIVE STRUCTURE
- Mzuzu Coffee Planters Cooperative Union (MCPCU): oversees 6 primary co-operative societies in northern Malawi; produces 400–450 metric tonnes/year.
- MCPCU exports globally via fixed contracts; handles marketing support since there is little government guidance on exporting.
- Farmers can earn 70–80% of final sales price through the co-op model (up from 20–30% under the old SCA).
- Co-ops offer Fairtrade and organic certification pathways, training in sustainable farming, and pest/disease management.
- Main export markets: South Africa, Germany, Japan, Holland, USA, UK. Domestic consumption: ~24 tonnes/year.

KEY CHALLENGES
- High crop mortality due to limited access to fertilizers and chemical inputs
- Coffee wilt disease (incurable once contracted; kills trees)
- Coffee berry disease, leaf rust, American leaf spot, stem borer beetles, antestia bugs
- Poor transport infrastructure in mountainous terrain; some farmers carry coffee by foot
- Competition from higher-volume neighboring origin Tanzania
- Rising labor costs making large-scale production more expensive
- More lucrative alternative crops (macadamia, tobacco, tea) drawing farmers away from coffee
- Production has fallen from 15,000 metric tonnes to ~1,000 metric tonnes annually

OUTLOOK
Despite challenges, stakeholders see potential:
- Government is increasing support to co-operatives
- Plans to expand co-ops into new growing regions: Dedza, Mangochi, Mchinji, Dowa, Ntcheu, Neno
- Existing growing areas are climatically suitable for quality coffee
- Farmers increasingly growing coffee in shaded areas to improve quality and reduce water use
- Quality from estate and cooperative coffee has room to grow toward specialty grade
