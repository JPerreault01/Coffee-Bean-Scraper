Coffee brewing methods fall into three mechanics: immersion, percolation, and pressure. Everything from a French press to an espresso machine is a variation on how water meets ground coffee and for how long. Get the method right for the bean and a cheap coffee tastes good. Get it wrong and a great coffee tastes flat.

This hub covers every method we brew for and review against: espresso, moka pot, French press, pour over, drip, AeroPress, Chemex, and cold brew. Each page explains the grind, ratio, and water temperature that method needs, the flavors it pulls forward, and which of our reviewed beans suit it best.

The short version: immersion methods like French press and cold brew give body and forgiveness. Percolation methods like pour over and drip give clarity and reward precision. Pressure methods like espresso and moka pot give intensity and crema but punish a bad grind. There is no single best brewer. There is the one that matches your beans, your time, and your morning. What are the main coffee brewing methods?

They group into three categories. Immersion steeps grounds directly in water, like a French press, cold brew, or AeroPress. Percolation passes water through a bed of grounds, like pour over, drip, and Chemex. Pressure forces hot water through a tight puck, like espresso and a moka pot.

Which coffee brewing method is best?

There is no objective best. Pour over gives the cleanest, brightest cup and rewards attention. French press gives the most body and forgives mistakes. Espresso gives intensity but needs equipment and practice. The best method is the one whose flavor and effort match what you actually want each morning.

What is the difference between French press and pour over?

Filtration and contact. A French press is full immersion through a metal mesh, so oils and fine sediment stay in, giving a heavy, rich cup. Pour over passes water through paper, which traps the oils and produces a clean, bright cup that highlights delicate notes. Same beans, very different texture.
