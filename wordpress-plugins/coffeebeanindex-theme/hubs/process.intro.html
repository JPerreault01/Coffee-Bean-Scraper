Coffee processing methods decide how much of the fruit's character ends up in the bean, and they change the cup as much as origin does. Processing is what happens between picking the cherry and shipping green coffee: how the fruit is removed, and how the bean is dried. The same Colombian beans can taste clean and bright when washed, or sweet and fruity when left natural.

This hub breaks down the four methods we track: washed, natural, honey, and wet-hulled. Each page explains what physically happens to the cherry, why it produces the flavor it does, and which reviewed beans use it.

The pattern is consistent. Washed strips all the fruit before drying, so the bean's own clarity and acidity lead. Natural dries the whole cherry, so sugars ferment into the bean and the cup turns fruity and heavy. Honey leaves part of the sticky mucilage on for a sweet middle ground. Wet-hulled, the Indonesian method behind Sumatra, hulls the bean wet and gives that earthy, low-acid weight. Pick a method below to see what it does to the cup.

What are the main coffee processing methods?

Three dominate, plus one regional method. Washed removes all the fruit before drying. Natural dries the whole cherry with the bean still inside. Honey leaves part of the sticky mucilage on during drying. Wet-hulled, used across Indonesia, hulls the bean at high moisture for an earthy, low-acid cup.

What is the difference between washed and natural coffee?

When the fruit comes off. Washed coffee is de-pulped and fermented clean before drying, so the cup is bright, clean, and acidity-forward. Natural coffee dries inside the whole cherry, so fruit sugars ferment into the bean, giving heavier body and bold berry sweetness. Washed shows the bean; natural shows the fruit.

Does honey processed coffee contain honey?

No. Honey process uses no honey at all. The name refers to the sticky, honey-like mucilage left clinging to the bean as it dries. That layer ferments slightly and adds sweetness and body, landing between a clean washed coffee and a fruity natural. Producers grade it yellow, red, or black by how much they leave on.
